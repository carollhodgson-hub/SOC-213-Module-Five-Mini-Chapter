❤️ Section 1: Relationship Pathways

People move through relationships in many different ways. Some remain single, some cohabit, some become engaged, some marry, and some move between different relationship statuses across the life course. Sociology helps us understand how these pathways are shaped by culture, economics, gender expectations, and opportunity.

🔍 Section 3: Why Relationships Succeed or Fail

Researchers have found that some couples who cohabit before marriage experience different outcomes than couples who do not. Sociologists debate whether cohabitation itself causes those outcomes or whether the characteristics of people who choose cohabitation explain the differences.

🧠 Correlation or Cause?

Select an explanation above.
Concept Connection:

The Selection Effect argues that people who choose cohabitation may already differ from those who do not. The Causal Cohabitation Effect argues that living together itself changes attitudes and behaviors in ways that influence relationship outcomes.

Sociologists often ask: Did cohabitation cause the outcome, or were the couples already different before they moved in together?

❤️ Section 8: Build a Better Message

Many communication problems occur because people express frustration through criticism, blame, or hostility rather than clearly communicating their needs. One strategy often taught in relationship education is the use of I-Statements.

❤️ Turn Criticism Into Communication

Choose a statement above.

📝 The I-Statement Formula

I feel ________
when ________
because ________
I would like ________

Why It Works:

I-Statements focus on feelings and needs rather than attacking another person's character. This reduces defensiveness and increases the likelihood of productive communication.
